Gelatine-Based Soft Sensors with Cellular Morphology

N. Tano, R. Hashem et al. — Soft Sensors / Morphological Design

A fundamental study of how mesoscale cellular morphology influences the sensing behaviour of soft, gelatine-based strain sensors. Rather than changing materials or electronics, this work explores how geometry alone can be used to tune sensitivity, directionality, and robustness.

  • Problem: Soft sensors often show unpredictable sensitivity and hysteresis, making it difficult to design sensors for specific tasks.
  • Approach: Introduce controlled cellular geometries (lattice, auxetic, honeycomb) into gelatine-based sensors and study their mechanical–electrical response.
  • Validation: Tensile testing, cyclic loading, and modelling to link morphology with resistance change under strain.
  • Outcome: Clear design rules showing how geometry can be used as a tuning parameter for soft sensor performance.

Why it matters

  • Provides design intuition for soft and stretchable sensors
  • Supports morphology-driven sensing in soft robotics
  • Forms a foundation for later wearable and robotic sensor systems
