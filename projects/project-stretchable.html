Wearable Stretchable Sensors — Practical Directions for Textile Integration

Lukas Cha, R. Hashem et al. — DIW + Embroidery; Naoki Tano, R. Hashem et al. — Mesoscale hydrogel morphologies

Two complementary research directions combined into a single wearable-sensors programme: (1) a scalable production route — multi-material direct-ink-writing of layered resistive sensors directly onto textiles with automated embroidery for strong mechanical and electrical integration; and (2) morphology-led sensitivity control — using mesoscale cellular patterns (lattice, auxetic, honeycomb) to tune sensitivity and directionality without changing chemistry.

  • Problem: Wearable sensors must be accurate, comfortable, durable, and manufacturable for real-world and sports settings.
  • Approach: Combine DIW + embroidered interconnects for robust production with morphology tuning (cell pattern/size/thickness) to set sensitivity and directional response.
  • Validation: Bench tensile cycles, on-body elbow/knee tests, and repeatability trials — current prototypes show promising linearity and useful joint-tracking accuracy for prototype systems.
  • Key result: A practical pathway to produce washable, textile-integrated sensors whose sensitivity is tuned by geometry (not just material), enabling fast designer iteration for sports and wearable applications.

Design directions (practical)

  • Choose morphology by goal: lattice for high sensitivity; auxetic for isotropy; honeycomb for softer feel.
  • Use cell size as a coarse knob: larger cells → higher sensitivity; smaller cells → more robust baseline behavior.
  • Production pairing: DIW-print multilayer pads onto garment → automated embroidery for perimeter anchoring and conductive leads → encapsulate with thin silicone for durability.
  • Placement for wearables: put high-sensitivity patches over joints (elbow, knee) and auxetic patches on regions needing bidirectional capture (shoulder, torso).
  • Sport science focus: target sampling, calibration and mounting to minimise motion artefacts; prioritize comfort, sweat resistance and washability for field trials.

Applications & next steps

  • Wearable joint tracking for sports performance and rehabilitation (training load, technique feedback).
  • Smart garments with localized patches for motion capture and injury prevention.
  • Roadmap: produce a combined prototype sleeve with lattice + auxetic patches, run a short sports-science pilot (10 users) to evaluate comfort, signal quality, and basic metric correlation vs optical motion capture.
