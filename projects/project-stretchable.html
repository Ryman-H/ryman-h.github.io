Wearable Stretchable Sensors — Hybrid Fabrication & Mesoscale Morphology

Lukas Cha, R. Hashem et al. — DIW + Embroidery (wearable resistive sensors, 2024); Naoki Tano, R. Hashem et al. — Mesoscale cellular hydrogel morphologies (design directions, 2024)

This project combines two complementary directions into a single wearable-sensors programme: (1) a scalable **hybrid fabrication** route — multi-material direct-ink-writing of an elastomer/conductor/elastomer stack directly onto textiles with simultaneous automated embroidery for mechanical anchoring and electrical interconnects; and (2) **mesoscale morphology design** — using in-plane cellular structures (lattice, auxetic, honeycomb, non-mesh) to tune sensitivity, directionality, and localization without changing chemistry. Together these routes give a practical, manufacturable pathway to tune sensitivity, robustness, and mapping for joint-tracking and tactile wearable skins.

  • Problem: Wearable strain sensors must be comfortable, durable, washable, and configurable for different sensitivity and directional needs — but existing routes either provide robust attachment (stitching) without multilayer printed stacks, or printed stacks without textile-grade interconnects or design-level sensitivity control.
  • Unified approach: Combine DIW-printed multilayer resistive stacks (silicone base → conductive carbon grease → silicone encapsulation) and automated embroidery (mechanical anchoring + conductive-thread traces) with mesoscale morphology (cellular meshes cast or printed) to achieve both manufacturing robustness and tunable sensing characteristics.
  • Fabrication & integration (what works): - DIW multilayer sensor printed directly onto textile (bed ~60°C) with 0.3 mm layers; total printed sensor ~20 minutes. - Automated embroidery (camera registration) stitches perimeter and conductive-thread interconnects in the same automated workflow, eliminating fragile discrete soldered connectors. - Mesoscale cellular sensors cast from gelatin/glycerol hydrogel into 3D-printed negatives (cell area: 25/50/100 mm²; thickness: 2–5 mm) or produced via DIW-compatible patterns for silicone composites.
  • Performance highlights: - DIW embroidered resistive sensors: R² ≈ 0.99 linearity, GF ≈ 31.4, hysteresis ≈ 22.9%, stretchability up to 120%, baseline drift ~0.135%/cycle over 80 cycles (see tensile-cyclic tests). - Mesoscale morphology: lattice cell increases GF (~+28% vs non-mesh), auxetic lowers GF (~−40% vs non-mesh); mesh area controls sensitivity magnitude; thickness affects durability but not GF.
  • Validation: Bench tensile testing, 80-cycle stability tests, stretch-to-failure measurements (DIW sensors) and FEM + UR3e robotic tensile rigs for hydrogel mesh characterization; on-body elbow & knee sleeve tests show usable joint-tracking (MAPE ≈ 17% with current prototypes — an acceptable baseline for prototype systems; next iterations target <10%).

Design directions — how to pick geometry & process

  • Pick morphology by target sensitivity: Lattice → maximize uniaxial GF (use for high-sensitivity joint patches). Auxetic → reduce GF and improve isotropy (use for bidirectional capture or reducing cross-talk). Honeycomb → softer mechanical feel with moderate GF. Use non-mesh as baseline for robust low-complexity sensors.
  • Use mesh area to tune GF magnitude: Larger mesh cells amplify morphology-driven GF differences; smaller cells converge toward non-mesh behaviour. Choose cell area as your coarse knob for sensitivity vs robustness. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
  • Process pairing for production: - For textile-first products: DIW-print the multilayer sensor directly to garment, then do positional-registered embroidery in the same production step (mechanical + electrical integration). This yields washable, reproducible assemblies. - For morphologically tuned hydrogel patches: cast hydrogel into printed negatives and encapsulate with thin silicone films to protect against moisture and abrasion before stitching/embedding into garments.
  • Electrical routing & contacts: Keep embroidered conductive-thread leads outside high-strain zones; anchor leads through reinforced stitch patterns and thin silicone channels to avoid contact delamination. Use LCR-based calibration connectors for field testing before finalizing sealed connectors.
  • Model-driven design: Use the spring-network + Pouillet model (convert mesh edges → springs → edge strains → per-edge RRC → total RRC) to predict sensor response before fabricating. This reduces iterations and helps choose mesh geometry for target GF and linear range.
  • Encapsulation trade-offs: Encapsulation improves wearability and washability but shifts hysteresis and GF. Measure GF after encapsulation and include that change in the model loop. For hydrogels, thin silicone encapsulation preserves response while improving durability.
